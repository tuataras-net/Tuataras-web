For years "microservices" was a synonym for "modern architecture". In 2026, the pendulum is swinging back. Serious teams — from Amazon Prime Video to Shopify — are publishing how they *consolidated* microservices back into monoliths. What happened? Let's unpack it.

## The original promise

- Independent deploys per team.
- Scale only what needs to scale.
- Fault isolation.
- Tech-stack flexibility per service.

All true — **if you have the size and maturity to sustain it**.

## The hidden cost

- **Operational complexity**: now you need service mesh, distributed tracing, secret management, multi-service canary deploys.
- **Network latency**: every internal call is now a TCP round-trip. What was O(1) in memory is O(n) over the wire.
- **Hard to debug**: a request crosses 14 services; finding the source of a 500 can take hours.
- **Infra cost**: 30 services = 30 images, 30 pipelines, 30 dashboards.

## When to pick a (modular) monolith

- Team has &lt;30 developers.
- Product is in validation or early growth.
- Traffic is manageable with vertical/horizontal process scaling.
- You don't have teams owning radically different domains.

**Trick**: *modular monolith* — single deployable, but with well-defined internal modules. Probably the most cost-effective architecture in 2026 for growing products.

## When microservices win

- 50+ developers organized by domain.
- Different parts of the system scale at very different rates.
- Compliance/legal needs requiring data isolation.
- You hit the monolith ceiling and release trains are painful.

## The winning hybrid pattern

Start as modular monolith. When a module grows enough or needs different scaling, **extract** it as a service. This is the Strangler Fig pattern, and it's the healthiest growth path.

## DORA metrics that matter

| Metric | Elite | High | Medium |
|---|---|---|---|
| Deployment frequency | Daily+ | Weekly | Monthly |
| Lead time for changes | &lt;1h | &lt;1 day | &lt;1 week |
| Change failure rate | 0–15% | 16–30% | 31–45% |
| MTTR | &lt;1h | &lt;1 day | &lt;1 week |

Your architecture is fine if you trend toward "elite" — regardless of mono or micro.

## Common anti-patterns

- **"Distributed monoliths"**: 14 services sharing one database. Not micro — distributed problems.
- **"Accidental monolith"**: started without design, now everything depends on everything. Refactor first, don't migrate to micro.
- **"Resume-driven architecture"**: choosing tech to pad CVs. Costs the business dearly.
