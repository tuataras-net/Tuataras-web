SRE was born at Google with huge teams. But the principles work at any scale — even if your "platform team" is just you. Here's the version adapted for 5–30 person teams.

## The 3 concepts that matter

### 1. SLO (Service Level Objective)
"Our API responds in &lt;500ms 99.5% of the time this quarter."
- A measurable promise.
- Agreed between product and eng.
- Starting point for everything else.

### 2. Error budget
If your SLO is 99.5%, your error budget is 0.5% downtime/month (~3.6h).
- Budget left → you can take risky changes, frequent deploys.
- Budget spent → freeze on non-critical changes until recovered.

### 3. Toil
Manual repetitive reactive work. SRE goal is &lt;50% time on toil.
- Cross that, automate more.
- Measure it: a sprint, log every hour what was toil.

## Minimal stack

- **Uptime monitoring**: Better Stack, Pingdom, Healthchecks.io.
- **Error tracking**: Sentry.
- **Logs**: Logtail, Axiom or cloud native.
- **Status page**: Statuspage.io or Better Stack.
- **On-call rotation**: Better Stack, PagerDuty, Opsgenie.

Total monthly for small team: $50–$300.

## On-call for 4–8 dev teams

- **Weekly rotation**, not short shifts (more tiring).
- **Only truly urgent alerts** wake people.
- **Clear compensation**: paid, time-off, or both.
- **Runbook per alert**: on-call shouldn't debug from scratch.
- **Always post-mortem**, blameless, with concrete action.

## Essential runbook

Per critical alert, document:
1. **What** the alert means.
2. **What impact** on customer.
3. **How to verify** it's real (not false positive).
4. **How to mitigate** fast (rollback, scale, kill).
5. **How to escalate** if you can't resolve in X min.

No runbook = every incident is anxiety from zero.

## Recommended practice: light chaos engineering

Once a quarter:
- Deliberately turn off a service in staging.
- See what fails, recovers, you find out late about.
- Improve what surfaced.

You don't need Chaos Monkey — `kubectl delete pod` or `aws ec2 terminate-instances` is enough.

## Common small-team mistakes

- Aspirational SLOs (99.99%) nobody can meet.
- Over-sensitive alerts that train you to ignore.
- No error budget → everything is priority → nothing is.
- Watching technical availability but not real UX (synthetic vs real).
