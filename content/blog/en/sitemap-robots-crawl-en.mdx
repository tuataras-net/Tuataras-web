60% of sites have errors in `robots.txt` or `sitemap.xml`. Most don't notice because it doesn't destroy SEO overnight — it silently drags it. Here's what to review and fix.

## What each does

### robots.txt
Tells crawlers **what NOT to crawl**. Lives at `yourdomain.com/robots.txt`.

### sitemap.xml
Tells Google **what you want considered**. List of important URLs with metadata.

**Not interchangeable**: robots.txt doesn't guarantee no indexing (only no crawling). To not index: `noindex` in HTML or headers.

## Minimum robots.txt structure

```
User-agent: *
Disallow: /admin/
Disallow: /api/internal/
Disallow: /*?session=

Sitemap: https://yourdomain.com/sitemap.xml
```

**Rules**:
- Start permissive, block only what makes sense.
- Blocking `?session` and similar avoids duplicate content.
- Explicitly list your sitemap.

## Typical mistakes

### 1. Blocking what you want indexed
Accidental `Disallow: /` destroys SEO completely. Happens more than you think when promoting staging to prod.

### 2. Assuming `Disallow` hides
No. If Google found URL via external link, it can index it even without crawling. To hide: `noindex` + no internal links.

### 3. Different robots between staging and prod
You upload staging robots to prod by accident. Watch the deploy.

### 4. Not testing in Search Console
There's a robots.txt tester in GSC. Use it before deploying.

## Sitemap.xml done right

### Size
Max **50,000 URLs or 50MB** per sitemap. Larger → split + sitemap index.

### Freshness
Dynamically generated with each change, not static-stale.

### Only canonical URLs
Don't include parameter variants, redirects, 404s.

### By type
Separate sitemaps per type (products, posts, static pages) ease diagnosis in Search Console.

### Well-structured example

```xml
<?xml version="1.0" encoding="UTF-8"?>
<urlset xmlns="http://www.sitemaps.org/schemas/sitemap/0.9">
  <url>
    <loc>https://yourdomain.com/blog/post-1</loc>
    <lastmod>2026-02-09</lastmod>
    <changefreq>weekly</changefreq>
    <priority>0.8</priority>
  </url>
</urlset>
```

`changefreq` and `priority` are weak hints — Google considers them little. **What matters**: `loc` and `lastmod`.

## Crawl budget: what and when it matters

**Crawl budget** = how many URLs Google is willing to crawl on your site in a period.

### When it matters
- **Large sites** (50k+ URLs).
- **Sites with lots of new content** needing fast indexing.
- **E-commerce** with many variants/filters.

### When it doesn't
- Sites &lt;1000 pages. Google crawls them all without problem.

### How to optimize it

1. **Block irrelevant** (filters with many combinations, empty tags, duplicated archive pages).
2. **Correct canonical** so Google knows preferred version.
3. **Smart internal linking**: important get more links.
4. **Fast 404s**: not 200 responses with "not found".
5. **Server speed**: Google crawls more if your server responds fast.

## Diagnosis with Search Console

**Crawl Stats** section: tells you crawled URLs, avg response time, file types.

**Pages** section: indexed vs non-indexed with reason. If "Discovered - currently not indexed" is high, quality or crawl budget issue.

## Real case

E-commerce with 80k URLs, 95% products. Only 22k indexed. Analysis:

- 30k URLs were combined filters without value.
- 8k were undetected 404s.
- Sitemap included tracking parameter URLs.

Actions:
- Block filters in robots.txt.
- Eliminate 404s or redirect to category.
- Sitemap only with clean canonical URLs.

**Result in 60 days**: indexing rose to 65k, organic traffic +52%, more efficient crawl rate.
