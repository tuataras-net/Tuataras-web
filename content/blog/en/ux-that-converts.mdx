Designing well is not about making something pretty. Designing well means getting users where they need to go — without friction, without confusion, and with the feeling that the product was made for them.

In this article, we share the principles we apply at Tuataras to create experiences that not only look good, but convert.

## The Most Common Mistake: Designing for Yourself

The biggest mistake internal teams make when designing their own products is assuming their users think like they do. They know the product, its shortcuts, its logic. The user doesn't.

The solution: real user research. Interviews, usability tests, heat maps. Nothing replaces watching a real person use your product.

## Visual Hierarchy: Most Important, First

The human eye follows predictable patterns when scanning a screen. Good design guides the gaze toward the action we want the user to take.

**Key principles:**
- Size and contrast to establish priority.
- Generous spacing to reduce cognitive load.
- A single primary CTA per screen — don't compete against yourself.

## Perceived Speed vs. Real Speed

A slow product frustrates. But a product that *feels* slow also frustrates, even if it's technically fast.

Skeleton screens, loading animations and immediate feedback on interaction are design techniques that improve the perception of speed without touching a single line of backend code.

## Consistency: The Silent Language of Design

When colors, typography, spacing and components are consistent throughout the product, users only have to learn once how it works. Every inconsistency is a moment of confusion.

A well-defined design system — even a simple one — is the foundation of a reliable experience.

## Measure to Improve

Design doesn't end at launch. Behavioral metrics (click rates, dwell times, abandonment rates) are continuous feedback that feeds the next improvement cycle.

A good UX team iterates constantly. Not because the design was wrong, but because it can always be better.
