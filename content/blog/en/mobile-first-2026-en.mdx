**70% of web traffic is mobile**. Google indexes the mobile version of your site first. Yet most sites are still "responsive" — which is not the same as mobile-first. Let's clarify.

## Responsive ≠ Mobile-first

**Responsive**: site adapts to smaller screens. Designed for desktop, compressed for mobile.

**Mobile-first**: site is designed for mobile FIRST and expands to desktop. Completely different philosophy.

The difference shows in hierarchy, simplicity, performance and conversion.

## The 7 most common mobile mistakes

1. **Hero with huge text and no visible CTA** without scrolling.
2. **Desktop forms shoved into mobile viewport**: 12 fields, wrong keyboard.
3. **Heavy images** taking 5s on 4G.
4. **Buttons &lt;44px** impossible to tap with thumb.
5. **Text &lt;16px**: mobile browser auto-zooms and breaks layout.
6. **Hamburger menus with 18 items** without hierarchy.
7. **Interstitial pop-ups** — Google penalizes directly.

## What works in 2026

### Sticky CTA
Main button always visible (fixed footer or header). Zero friction to convert.

### Bottom navigation pattern
Instead of hamburger, bottom nav with 4–5 key items. Pattern inspired by native apps.

### Correct native inputs
- `type="email"` → keyboard with @
- `type="tel"` → numeric keyboard
- `inputmode="decimal"` for prices

### Brutal performance
LCP &lt;2.5s on real 4G, not studio wifi. Modern image formats (WebP/AVIF), minimal JS above the fold.

### Gesture-aware UX
Horizontal swipe for galleries, pull-to-refresh where it makes sense. Native patterns respected.

## The thumb test

Hold your phone with one hand. Try reaching every critical element (CTA, nav, search) without repositioning. If you can't, your site fails the thumb test.

## The new metric: INP

**INP** (Interaction to Next Paint) replaced FID in 2024 as Core Web Vital. Measures how fast your site responds to clicks, taps, inputs. Target: &lt;200ms.

JS-heavy sites usually fail this. It's where most money is lost unknowingly.
